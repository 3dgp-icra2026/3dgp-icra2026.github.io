Abstract

Recent advances in robotic manipulation have highlighted the effectiveness of learning from demonstration. However, while end-to-end policies excel in expressivity and flexibility, they struggle with generalization. An alternative approach of object-centric prediction offers improved generalizability but often relies on restrictive assumptions regarding object rigidity and unimodal scene placements. In this work, we introduce a hierarchical goal-prediction framework for object placement that overcomes these limitations. Specifically, we model global scene-level placements through a novel Dense Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM); we model local object-level configuration through a disentangled reference diffusion and shape diffusion. By decoupling placement prediction into these two stages, our method generalizes effectively to scene multi-modality while supporting high-precision and non-rigid placements. We validate our approach across a suite of challenging tasks, demonstrating strong performance in both rigid and non-rigid settings in simulation and in the real world.

Model Architecture

Global Placement Initialization

Our model initializes a rough global position using a novel dense GMM-based prediction module, a framework that models highly multi-modal placement distributions at the scene-level.

Local Configuration Refinement

Our model then proceeds with local configuration refinement, in which we introduce a disengantled shape diffusion and reference frame diffusion that simultaneously allow precise and dense (i.e. non-rigid) goal predictions.

Real World Results - NIST Board Insertion Tasks

Insertion After Human Handoffs

We show that our method enables reliable human-robot collaborative manipulation by performing connector insertions after human handoffs. Specifically, a human hands a connector (Waterproof, DSUB-25, or SSD) with arbitrary pose directly to a robot arm, which then autonomously performs precise insertion into a designated socket on the NIST Assembly Task Board, based on the goal configurations predicted by our method. This demonstrates that our goal prediction framework exhibits strong generalization to unseen object configurations with random occlusions due to varying view points, while maintaining millimeter-scale precision required for successful real-world insertions.


Insertion from Randomized Initial Poses

We further evaluate our method both qualitatively and quantitatively across three connectors (Waterproof, DSUB-25, and SSD), each with 20 insertion trials involving randomly sampled rotations and translations (see Appendix for details). To enable quantitative evaluation, the robot autonomously grasps the plug at a random angle variation between -10Â° and 10Â° and random translation variation between -5mm and 5mm across feasible axes, removes it from the socket, and moves to a default pose, discarding the grasp orientation. A point cloud of the plug and socket is then captured, and our and the baseline method predicts the appropriate insertion configuration without access to the original ground truth. This contrasts with the previous human-handoff setting, where the initial pose is unknown and quantitative error computation is not feasible. While our framework is designed to handle both rigid and non-rigid objects and supports multi-modal placement goals, our real-world experiment demonstrates that it also excels in rigid, unimodal goal prediction settings. We further explore multi-modal placement and non-rigid goal prediction in our simulation experiments.
